The One-Sentence Positioning Test (Most Founders Fail This)

If you can't explain what you do in one clear sentence — not a tagline, not a mission statement, one sentence — your brand has a positioning problem. Let's fix it.

15 min read 1 exercise Free — no email required

Why one sentence is harder than a paragraph

Here is a thing that happens at almost every brand audit I run. I ask a founder: "What does your company do?" They take a breath. Then they give me two minutes of product features, a market trend, a mention of their tech stack, a story about their first customer, and something about disrupting an industry.

None of that is positioning. It's product knowledge. There's a difference.

Positioning is not about explaining everything you do. It is about owning a specific, understood place in a person's mind. The second someone hears what you do, they need to file you somewhere — and that filing needs to happen fast, or they bounce.

Positioning is a filing system inside your customer's brain. You don't control the brain. You only control the label.

When founders struggle to give me a one-sentence answer, it usually means one of three things: they're trying to serve too many customers at once, they haven't decided what makes them different from alternatives, or they've been writing about their product so long they've lost the ability to see it from the outside.

All three are fixable. But you have to diagnose which one you have first.

The test itself

This is the test. Write one sentence that completes this structure:

The Positioning Sentence Formula

[Your product] helps [specific customer] [do/achieve/avoid specific thing] [without/unlike/so that — the key differentiator].

That's it. Four parts. Let's go through each one.

Your product. Name it. Just the name. Not the category, not the vision. The thing.

Specific customer. Not "businesses" or "people who want to grow." The more specific you get here, the better the sentence performs. "Early-stage SaaS founders with a product team of one" beats "software companies" every time. Specificity signals you understand someone. Generality signals you're afraid of excluding someone.

Specific outcome. What does the customer actually walk away with? Not a feature — an outcome. "Reduce churn" is an outcome. "Analytics dashboard" is a feature. Outcomes are what people pay for. Features are how you deliver them.

The differentiator. This is the part most founders either skip or get wrong. The differentiator is not "we're better" — everyone says that. It's a specific contrast: without needing a developer, unlike spreadsheets, so that you don't need to hire a consultant, in under 30 minutes. It should make someone think "oh, that's actually different from what I currently use."

What failing the test looks like

Most failed attempts fall into recognizable patterns. Here are three you've probably written before.

"We help businesses grow with data-driven insights." This fails on customer (too broad), outcome (growth is not an outcome, it's a desire), and differentiator (data-driven insights is the promise of every SaaS product built since 2010). Replace every word with a more specific word and this sentence transforms.

"The all-in-one platform for modern teams." All-in-one means nothing. Modern means nothing. Teams means nothing. This sentence is not positioning — it's a mood board in sentence form.

"We're like Slack but for [insert niche]." Comparative positioning built on someone else's brand is fragile. What happens when Slack adds a feature for your niche? Your positioning dissolves overnight. Anchor to your own specific value, not a competitor's category.

Quick diagnostic

Read your sentence, then ask: could a direct competitor paste their logo onto this sentence and have it still be true? If yes, your differentiator isn't specific enough. Keep going until the answer is no.

Passing the test — and what to do with it

A sentence that passes the test feels slightly uncomfortable to write. That's a feature, not a bug. Narrow positioning feels like you're leaving people out. You are. That's the whole point.

The founders I've worked with who resist specificity almost always give me the same reason: "I don't want to exclude potential customers." But vague positioning doesn't attract more customers — it attracts no customers, because no one identifies with it strongly enough to act.

Narrow positioning converts better. Specific people read it and think "this is for me." That's the only thought you need to trigger on a landing page. Everyone else was never your customer anyway.

Once you have a sentence that passes the test, it becomes your messaging anchor. Every headline, every email subject line, every sales call opener should be derivable from that one sentence. If someone asks your SDR what the company does, the answer should rhyme with your positioning sentence. If it doesn't, you have a messaging consistency problem — which is a separate but related issue we get into in course four.

Your exercise

Write your positioning sentence right now using the formula above. Don't spend more than five minutes on a first draft. Then apply the competitor paste test — could a competitor use this sentence with their logo? If yes, sharpen the differentiator.

Do it three times. The first version is what you think your positioning is. The second version is usually more honest. The third version is the one you'll actually use.

Target: a sentence under 25 words that a stranger could repeat back to you after reading it once.
