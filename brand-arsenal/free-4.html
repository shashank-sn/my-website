Three Brand Mistakes Killing Your Conversion Rate

Before you hire a CRO consultant or redesign your landing page, read this. Most conversion problems have a brand positioning root cause — and the fix costs nothing but clarity.

18 min read 1 audit checklist Free — no email required

The wrong diagnosis is costing you money

When conversion rates are low, founders go straight to the tactical layer. They run A/B tests on button colors. They shorten the form. They hire someone to add a chatbot. They redesign the hero section three times. They try a new headline generator.

None of it moves the needle — because the problem isn't the button, the form, or the headline font. The problem is that a visitor landed on the page, couldn't quickly understand what you do, why it's different, and whether it's for them — and left. That's a brand positioning failure. CRO tools can optimize a page that has a clear message. They cannot manufacture clarity that isn't there.

A/B testing a confusing page just tells you which version of confusion performs slightly less badly.

Here are the three positioning mistakes I see most consistently across the companies I audit — and the specific fix for each one.

01

You're writing for yourself, not your customer

This is the most common and most invisible mistake. It shows up as hero copy that's heavy on what the product is and light on what the customer gets. It shows up as feature lists presented in the order your engineering team built them, not the order your customer cares about. It shows up as a "How it works" section that describes your internal process instead of the customer's transformation.

The technical name for this is founder-perspective messaging. You're so close to your product that you've lost the ability to read your own page as a stranger. You know what every phrase means. Your visitor doesn't — and they're not going to work for it.

The test: read every sentence on your homepage and ask "is this about us or about them?" A sentence like "We built an AI-powered pipeline using proprietary NLP models" is about you. A sentence like "Know exactly which leads are ready to buy — before you make the call" is about them. Your page should be overwhelmingly the second kind.

The fix
Rewrite your hero section entirely from the customer's point of view. Start with the problem they have right now, before they found you. Name it specifically. Then introduce your product as the resolution. Never lead with technology or methodology — lead with the situation your customer is already in.
02

Your messaging is inconsistent across touchpoints

A visitor clicks on a paid ad that says "The fastest way for freelancers to get paid on time." They land on a homepage hero that says "Financial tools for modern independents." They click through to the pricing page, which talks about "streamlined invoicing for growing businesses." Three different audiences, three different value propositions, one product.

This is messaging inconsistency — and it destroys trust in a way that's hard to diagnose because no single touchpoint looks wrong. The ad is fine. The homepage is fine. The pricing page is fine. But the customer's brain experienced three different companies in the space of 90 seconds, and their subconscious decided something is off.

Inconsistent messaging is almost always a symptom of positioning work that was never fully committed to. Someone wrote good copy for the ad. Someone else wrote the homepage. A third person wrote the pricing page. Nobody had a positioning sentence from Course 1 to anchor everything to.

The fix
Audit every customer-facing touchpoint — ad copy, landing page, email sequences, pricing page, onboarding flow — and check whether the core positioning claim is consistent across all of them. The exact words don't need to be identical. The audience, the primary benefit, and the differentiator should be unmistakably the same throughout.
03

You're not giving visitors a clear reason to choose you over the thing they're already doing

Every purchase decision is actually a switching decision. Your visitor is already doing something — using a competitor, using a spreadsheet, using nothing and living with the problem. Your page needs to make one thing absolutely clear: why switching to you is worth the cost and the friction.

Most pages never address this directly. They describe the product enthusiastically. They list features. They show social proof. But they never look the visitor in the eye and say "here's exactly why what you're using right now isn't solving this — and here's why we do." That directness feels risky to founders. It feels presumptuous or aggressive. But from the visitor's perspective, the page that acknowledges their current situation and makes the case for changing it is the page that feels like it was written for them.

The best version of this isn't a competitor takedown. It's an acknowledgment of what existing solutions get wrong about the problem — followed immediately by your mechanism from Course 2. "Most tools do X. We do Y instead, because [reason that makes Y obviously better for your specific customer]."

The fix
Add a section to your homepage — or a block in your hero copy — that explicitly names the status quo your customer is living with, names what's broken about it, and positions your product as the specific alternative. You don't need to name competitors. Name the behavior or the tool type: "If you're still doing this in spreadsheets..." or "Most [category] tools make you [frustrating thing]. We built [product] so you never have to [frustrating thing] again."
Quick gut-check

Give your homepage to someone who has never heard of your product — not a friend, someone genuinely outside your network. Ask them to read it and then answer: What does this product do? Who is it for? Why would someone use this instead of what they're currently using? If they can't answer all three clearly, you have at least one of the three mistakes above.

Your audit checklist

Go through your homepage right now and check for each mistake:

Mistake 1 check: Count the sentences on your hero section. How many start with "We" or reference your product/company? Every sentence starting with "We" needs to be rewritten to start with the customer's situation or outcome.

Mistake 2 check: Copy your homepage headline, your most-run ad headline, and your email subject line from your last campaign. Read them side by side. Do they describe the same product to the same customer with the same core promise? If not, which one is most accurate — and make the others match.

Mistake 3 check: Does your page ever directly name what your customer is currently doing instead of using your product? If not, write one paragraph that does. Put it directly after your hero section as a test.
