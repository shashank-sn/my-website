How to Make People Remember What You Do

Most founders mistake recognition for memorability. Recognition is "I've seen that logo before." Memorability is "I knew exactly who to call when this problem came up." Only one of them drives revenue.

20 min read 1 exercise Free — no email required

Why smart people forget you immediately

You had a great call. The prospect was engaged, asked smart questions, said "this is exactly what we've been looking for." Three weeks later you follow up and they say "sorry, remind me what you do again?"

It's not that they're rude or forgetful. It's that their brain received your positioning as generically as it receives every other SaaS pitch — and generic information does not get assigned a dedicated storage slot. It gets filed under "misc software I heard about" and competes with everything else in that folder for retrieval.

This is a positioning failure, not a memory failure. The brain is extraordinarily good at remembering things that are specific, surprising, or connected to a strong existing mental category. The brain is terrible at remembering things that are vague, incremental, or require active effort to decode. Most startup positioning lands squarely in the second category.

People don't remember what you do. They remember how you made them see a problem differently.

Memorability is an engineering problem. There are specific mechanisms that make information sticky — and you can deliberately build them into your positioning, your pitch, and your homepage copy. Here are the three most effective ones.

The three memorability mechanisms

Mechanism 01
The Reframe — Make them see the problem differently

The most memorable brands don't just describe what they do. They change how you think about the problem they solve. This is called a reframe, and it works because the human brain assigns deep storage to moments of genuine new understanding.

Reframes usually take the form of "you thought the problem was X, but it's actually Y." They work when Y is genuinely surprising but, once heard, obviously true. They fail when Y is just a rephrasing of the same idea with different words.

A good reframe sounds like: "Most companies think they have a lead generation problem. They actually have a clarity problem — because 70% of leads who don't convert do so because they couldn't quickly understand what the product did." The reframe shifts the diagnosis. Once someone hears it, they can't un-hear it — and they remember who told them.

How to find your reframe

Ask yourself: what does my typical customer believe about this problem before they find us — and what do they understand after working with us? The gap between those two beliefs is your reframe. Write it as a "you think X, but actually Y" statement. If it makes someone nod slowly and say "huh" — you have it.

Mechanism 02
The Anchor — Give them a mental hook to hang you on

The brain stores new information by attaching it to something already known. This is why analogies and comparisons are so powerful — not as a crutch, but as an encoding mechanism. When you give someone a clear, accurate anchor, you're essentially filing yourself in their brain for them.

The most common version of this is the "[Famous Company] for [Specific Niche]" format. It's overused and often lazy — but the underlying mechanic is sound. The problem with most executions is the anchor is too broad ("Slack for enterprises") rather than specific enough to own a distinct slot ("the tool Notion users build when they outgrow Notion").

A stronger version of the anchor technique is what I call the enemy frame: instead of anchoring to a similar company, anchor to the thing you replace. "The spreadsheet your team actually abandons" or "what happens after Calendly stops working at scale." You're borrowing the mental real estate of something already stored in the prospect's brain and marking your territory next to it.

The anchor doesn't have to be a competitor. It can be a behavior, a tool category, a frustration, or a moment. The question is: what is already living in your customer's head that your product belongs next to?

Mechanism 03
The Specific Number — Replace vague claims with precise ones

Specificity creates credibility and aids memory in equal measure. "We help companies grow faster" disappears immediately. "We reduced onboarding time from 11 days to 3 for companies with 50-person sales teams" sticks — because it's too precise to be fabricated, it's concrete enough to visualize, and it's attached to a specific type of customer your prospect can identify with.

The mistake founders make with specificity is thinking it narrows their appeal. The opposite is true. Specific claims feel more believable than vague ones. A visitor reading "reduces churn by 30%" is more likely to believe it and act on it than a visitor reading "significantly improves retention." The specificity signals that you've measured, that you know your customer deeply, and that you're confident enough in the result to name it.

You don't need a perfect stat. You need a honest one. If your best customer reduced their time-to-close by 40%, that's your number. If the average onboarding takes 2 days instead of the industry standard of 2 weeks, that's your number. Find the most compelling specific truth about your product's impact and put it in your positioning — not buried in a case study, in the hero.

Putting it together: the memorable positioning sentence

Go back to the positioning sentence you wrote in Course 1. Now run it through these three filters:

Reframe filter: Does this sentence contain a perspective shift? Does it tell the customer something genuinely surprising about their problem or the solution category? If not, add a reframe clause: "Unlike [what they currently believe], [your product] works by [mechanism]."

Anchor filter: Is there a clear mental hook in this sentence? Something the customer's brain can attach to existing knowledge? If not, add a reference to the thing you replace, improve upon, or make obsolete.

Specificity filter: Is there at least one number or concrete detail in your positioning or your supporting copy? If not, identify the most honest specific result your product delivers and add it within the first screen of your homepage.

You don't have to hit all three in a single sentence. One is enough to move from forgettable to memorable. Two makes you the company someone thinks of first when the problem comes up in conversation. All three makes you the obvious reference point in your category.

The word-of-mouth test

The ultimate test of memorability is word-of-mouth accuracy. Ask someone who's heard your pitch to describe your company to a third person right now — not in a week, right now. If what they say bears close resemblance to your positioning sentence, you've done it. If it's a vague paraphrase that could describe twelve other companies, you have more work to do. The goal is a positioning so clear and specific that it survives the telephone game intact.

Your final exercise

Take your best current positioning statement — the one you'd use on a homepage hero or a first sales call. Run it through all three filters:

1. Reframe: What is the counter-intuitive truth at the heart of what you do? Write it as "Most people think [X]. The real problem is [Y]." Then see if that insight can be folded into your positioning.

2. Anchor: What does your product make obsolete, replace, or dramatically improve upon? Name it directly. Use it as a contrast in your positioning sentence.

3. Specific number: What is the most compelling, honest, specific result your product has delivered? Put it in your hero section within the next 24 hours.

Then: say your revised positioning sentence out loud to someone right now. Ask them to repeat it back in their own words 5 minutes later. That's your memorability score.
